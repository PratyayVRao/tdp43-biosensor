01 — The Diagnostic Gap

Neurons lost to disease never return.

ALS, frontotemporal dementia, and Alzheimer's share one property: irreversibility. No therapy restores a neuron that has already died. The only variable left to control is how early disease is caught. Today's diagnostic tools are expensive, invasive, and often months too slow.

PATIENT AMarco
2 weeks to referral

Receives a blood draw analyzed by a SATA-bioconjugated nanobiosensor as part of a clinical trial. Elevated TDP-43 (0.8 ng/mL) flagged well below the threshold conventional ELISA could reliably catch.

Begins neuroprotective treatment and enrolls in a targeted ALS trial. Two years later, progression is significantly slowed.
PATIENT BElena
16 months to diagnosis

Standard bloodwork returns normal. No biomarker test is available at her facility. She is reassured and sent home as symptoms quietly progress.

By confirmation via lumbar puncture, an estimated 30% of spinal motor neurons are already lost.
PATIENT CJames
8 months on a waitlist

Lives three hours from the nearest academic medical center. His family doctor suspects something neurological but has no fast-track referral network.

Diagnosed with FTD and confirmed TDP-43 pathology. A local point-of-care test could have changed the trajectory.

03 — The Chemistry

What bioconjugation does

An antibody recognizes one target but cannot generate an electrical signal. A gold electrode generates a signal but cannot identify proteins. Bioconjugation chemically links them into a single functioning sensor.

Homobifunctional vs. heterobifunctional

Crosslinkers are molecular bridges that connect an antibody to the electrode surface by targeting reactive groups on the antibody: primary amines on lysine residues, thiols on cysteine residues.

Symmetric

Homobifunctional

Identical reactive groups at both ends. No control over which direction the antibody faces once attached.

Directional · used here

Heterobifunctional

Different reactive groups at each end. Enables step-wise attachment that keeps the antibody's binding site facing outward.

03 — Reaction Pathways

Three ways to make the bond

Each approach trades speed, control, and cost differently. This biosensor uses SATA thiol modification.

NHS Ester / Amine Coupling

N-hydroxysuccinimide esters react with primary amines on the antibody surface under mild alkaline conditions, forming stable amide bonds. Fast and efficient, but lysines are distributed across the whole antibody surface, including near the binding site. Some conjugated antibodies end up facing the wrong direction.

pH window7.2 – 9.0
Site controlLow
Bond typeAmide
SpeedFast

SATA Thiol Modification

SATA reacts with antibody amines to install a protected thiol group. Hydroxylamine deprotection liberates a free sulfhydryl, which forms a spontaneous gold-sulfur (Au-S) bond directly with the electrode. This produces denser surface coverage and more consistent antibody orientation than NHS coupling.

Bond formedAu–S
Surface coverageHigh / dense
Cost per sensor$2.10
Site controlHigh

Click Chemistry (CuAAC / SPAAC)

Azide and alkyne groups react to form a stable triazole ring under aqueous conditions with no unwanted byproducts. The copper-free SPAAC variant eliminates cytotoxic copper entirely. Because azides and alkynes don't occur naturally in biological samples, the reaction targets only the intended partner.

Bond formedTriazole
ByproductsNone
BioorthogonalYes
CatalystCu-free (SPAAC)
04 — Construction

Build the sensor, layer by layer

Four layers, each with a specific function. The same antibody-antigen selectivity from Proof 1 is anchored here with a covalent Au-S bond in place of a hand.

  • 01
    Gold electrode substrate

    Conductive and biocompatible. Gold's affinity for thiol-containing molecules via Au-S bond formation makes it the foundation every other layer builds on.

  • 02
    Self-assembled monolayer (SAM)

    A uniform thiol layer that primes the surface and blocks non-specific protein adsorption. Without it, background serum proteins would bind randomly and produce false signal.

  • 03
    Bioconjugated antibody layer

    SATA-thiolated anti-TDP-43 antibodies form Au-S bonds with the gold surface, with binding sites oriented outward and accessible to the sample.

  • 04
    BSA blocking layer

    Bovine serum albumin fills exposed gold gaps between antibodies. Chemically inert, it ensures only TDP-43 binding generates a signal.

Stack status: Layer 1 of 4 active
05 — Detection Mechanism

From a bound protein to a number

In ALS and FTD, TDP-43 mislocalizes from the nucleus, misfolds, and leaks into the bloodstream as neurons break down. That makes it detectable in a blood draw.

Differential pulse voltammetry

When serum is applied, TDP-43 binds the anchored antibodies. The binding changes the electrode interface: added mass and shifted charge alter how the electrode responds to an applied voltage.

A series of small voltage pulses is applied and the resulting current measured. The shift maps directly to TDP-43 concentration via a calibration curve, producing a result in under an hour.

This is the number Marco's doctor had and James's didn't. The same pathology that eventually produces the lesion in Proof 2 is detectable here, in blood, months earlier.
